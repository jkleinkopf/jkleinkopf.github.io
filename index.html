About

I am currently a science teacher with North Thurston Public Schools. I completed my M.Ed. at UMass-Boston and teacher training through the Boston Teacher Residency. Previously, I completed my M.S. in the Roalson Lab at Washington State University and my B.A. at the University of Colorado.

Teaching Interests

Utilizing culturally relevant pedagogy and phenomenon- and project-based learning are my main teaching interests. As a science teacher, I am particularly interested in finding ways to connect scientific concepts to students' cultural backgrounds and physical surroundings. The PNW has been a great place for teaching geology and biology through ties to the region's cultures and physical surroundings.

Research Interests

Broadly speaking, I am interested in using phylogenetic tools to resolve species relationships in young plant lineages. In particular, my masters work in the Roalson Lab focused on using Hyb-Seq, a relatively new phylogenomic approach, to better understand relationships within the Hawaiian Cyrtandra. The approach resulted in phylogenies in line with morphological hypotheses, and may help us better understand species relationships of the group with wider sampling in the future.

In the Tripp Lab at CU-Boulder, I focused on morphologically aberrant populations of plants from the Purgatory Watershed, and testing to see if molecular data from these species corroborates the morphological differences between populations. The aberrant population of one species, Amorpha nana, harbors both molecular signals that indicate this population has been isolated from non-aberrant populations of this species.
