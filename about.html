About Joseph

I grew up in Boulder, CO. and attended college at the University of Colorado, where I majored in molecular biology and Japanese language. In my final year at CU, I learned about the staggering diversity of plants and began conducting my own research on plant diversity. With Dr. Tripp and Dina Clark, I explored the Purgatory Watershed in southeastern Colorado for evidence that the area harbors high levels of biodiversity, and found that at least one Purgatory plant species, Amorpha nana, exhibits morphological and molecular characteristics distinctly different from the A. nana in other places across the West.

In 2016, I moved to the inland Pacific Northwest to study patterns of plant evolution in Dr. Roalson's lab at Washington State University. My Master's thesis explored how the genus Cyrtandra on Hawaii has evolved. I used next-gen sequencing technology and species-tree approaches to phylogeny reconstruction in order to estimate the evolutionary history of Hawaiian Cyrtandra, and found that the molecular data largely correlates to morphological characters observed for the genus. Though there is more work to be done, my work at WSU shows that species-tree reconstruction approaches are appropriate for young lineages on Hawaii, and is likely to give us insight into the history of this and other groups of plants!

I am currently in training to become a teacher and am completing my teacher education at UMass Boston in the Boston Teacher Residency program. I intend to develop project-based biology/AP biology curricula that explore biology concepts and uncover how social inequity has shaped (and is shaping) science today. Pedagogically, the curricula I hope to develop will be culturally responsive, student-centered, and highly differentiated. Additionally, I am interested in implementing the science writing heuristic in my curricula so that students develop their ability to analyze their own learning through written argument and reflection.
